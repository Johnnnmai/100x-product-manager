## Purpose

Guide product managers through a sharp, high-signal teardown of a resume against a specific target role. Use this to identify why a PM resume is not converting, separate weak bullets from strong evidence, and produce a prioritized rewrite plan that improves interview odds without turning the resume into buzzword soup.

This is not a generic resume template generator. It is a diagnostic and rewrite process focused on PM hiring signal: scope, business impact, decision quality, leadership, and role fit.

## Key Concepts

### What Good PM Resume Signal Looks Like

A strong PM resume usually answers five questions fast:

1. **What level are you really operating at?**
   - Feature PM
   - Senior PM
   - Staff / Lead PM
   - Director-track PM

2. **What outcomes did you move?**
   - Revenue
   - Retention
   - Activation
   - Margin / efficiency
   - Strategic risk reduction

3. **How complex was the problem?**
   - Size of customer/problem
   - Cross-functional scope
   - Decision ambiguity
   - Constraints and trade-offs

4. **What was your actual contribution?**
   - What you owned
   - What you influenced
   - What you supported but did not lead

5. **Why should this employer care?**
   - Target role fit
   - Domain relevance
   - Stage relevance
   - Story coherence

### The Resume Teardown Scorecard

Score the resume across five lenses:

| Lens | What strong looks like | Common failure mode |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Target clarity** | Clear role direction and level | Looks like a generic PM document |
| **Business impact** | Metrics tied to real outcomes | Activity without measurable effect |
| **Scope & complexity** | Shows size, ambiguity, cross-functional weight | Bullets feel too tactical or too small |
| **Ownership** | Clear statement of your role | Sounds like team output, not your contribution |
| **Signal-to-noise** | High-value bullets lead, fluff is cut | Too many tools, process words, or filler |

### Anti-Patterns

- **Task lists disguised as impact**: "Wrote PRDs, managed backlog, aligned stakeholders"
- **Metrics without context**: Big numbers with no baseline, scope, or why they mattered
- **Leadership inflation**: Claiming ownership you cannot defend in interviews
- **Keyword stuffing**: Adding AI, strategy, growth, or zero-to-one everywhere without proof

### When to Use This

- Before a PM job search starts
- Before applying to a new PM role cluster
- When a resume is getting low interview conversion
- When switching from one PM narrative to another (e.g. platform -> growth, startup -> big tech)

### When Not to Use This

- When you need design/layout help more than content help
- When you have no target role in mind at all
- When your problem is interview performance, not resume quality

## Application

This interactive skill asks **up to 4 adaptive questions**, then delivers a prioritized teardown with rewrite guidance.

### Step 0: Gather Inputs

Ask the user to provide:
- target role(s)
- target company type or stage
- current resume or top 6-10 bullets
- any known weak point (low recruiter response, weak metrics, unclear positioning)

If they do not have a target role, ask them to pick a default lane:
1. Core product / feature PM
2. Growth PM
3. Platform / infra PM
4. AI / agentic PM

### Question 1: What Role Are We Optimizing For?

Ask:

"Which role are you trying to convert for right now?"

1. **Senior PM** - stronger execution + outcome ownership
2. **Staff / Lead PM** - larger scope, more ambiguity, more influence
3. **Growth PM** - funnels, experimentation, retention, monetization
4. **AI / platform / technical PM** - systems thinking, technical depth, cross-functional complexity

Or let the user describe a specific title and company type.

### Question 2: What Is the Main Conversion Problem?

Ask:

"What is the biggest failure mode of the current resume?"

1. **Too tactical** - reads like tasks instead of decisions and outcomes
2. **Too generic** - could fit any PM job, but signals no sharp strength
3. **Too weak on metrics** - impact is vague or hard to trust
4. **Too noisy** - too much filler, too many bullets, too many tools

### Question 3: What Is Your Strongest Evidence?

Ask:

"Which kind of proof do you actually have the most of?"

1. **Growth / monetization wins**
2. **Complex cross-functional launches**
3. **Customer discovery / strategy work**
4. **Operational / platform / systems improvements**

Use this to decide what the resume should lead with, rather than trying to tell every story equally.

### Question 4: What Constraint Matters Most?

Ask:

"What constraint should shape the rewrite?"

1. **Need results fast** - improve current resume without rewriting everything
2. **Changing lanes** - reposition for a different PM role cluster
3. **Low evidence density** - make modest wins sound credible, not inflated
4. **Need interview support too** - resume and stories must line up

### Output

Generate all of the following:

1. **Resume scorecard**
   - Target clarity
   - Business impact
   - Scope & complexity
   - Ownership
   - Signal-to-noise

2. **Top 5 fixes in priority order**
   - What to change first
   - Why it matters
   - What not to do

3. **Bullet-level rewrite guidance**
   - 3-5 rewritten bullets
   - keep them truthful and interview-defensible

4. **Narrative positioning**
   - one-line professional headline
   - 2-3 themes the resume should emphasize
   - 2-3 themes to reduce or cut

5. **Next-step recommendation**
   - if the resume is the blocker, say so
   - if interview stories are the blocker, point the user to `pm-mock-interview-workflow`
   - if role targeting is the blocker, point the user to `bigtech-vs-startup-decision-advisor`

## Examples

### Example Scenario

**Input:** A PM wants senior-growth roles but the resume mostly describes shipping features and stakeholder alignment.

**Good output shape:**
- diagnose that the role target is growth, but the evidence stack is feature execution
- recommend leading with activation, retention, experiment, monetization, and funnel language only where truthfully supported
- rewrite bullets from task language to outcome language
- cut generic PM verbs that burn precious resume space

### Example Rewrite Pattern

**Weak bullet:**
> Owned onboarding redesign and partnered with design and engineering to improve the user experience.

**Stronger bullet:**
> Led onboarding redesign across product, design, and engineering, increasing activation from 41% to 57% by simplifying first-run setup and removing two drop-off steps.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Optimizing for Every Role at Once

**Symptom:** The resume tries to look like growth PM, platform PM, AI PM, and zero-to-one PM simultaneously.

**Fix:** Pick a primary lane for this version of the resume.

### Pitfall 2: Making Bullets Sound Bigger Than They Were

**Symptom:** The rewritten bullet sounds impressive but cannot survive an interview follow-up.

**Fix:** Only increase clarity and signal. Do not invent ownership, strategy, or impact.

### Pitfall 3: Leading with Process Instead of Results

**Symptom:** The first impression is backlog grooming, ceremonies, PRDs, and stakeholder syncs.

**Fix:** Lead with the outcome, the problem, or the decision quality.

### Pitfall 4: Keeping Low-Signal Bullets for Emotional Reasons

**Symptom:** Resume is crowded because every project feels hard-earned.

**Fix:** Keep only what improves the target-role narrative.
